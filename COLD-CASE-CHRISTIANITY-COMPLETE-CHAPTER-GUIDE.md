## Book Overview

J. Warner Wallace, a cold-case homicide detective, applies his investigative skills to examine the claims of Christianity. He treats the Gospels as "cold cases" and uses forensic principles to determine if they are reliable.

---

## Part 1: The Nature of Evidence

### Chapter 1: A Detective's Approach to Faith
Wallace introduces his investigative approach to faith. As a detective, he learned to examine evidence carefully before drawing conclusions. He applies the same principles to the claims of Christianity.

**Key Points:**
- The importance of evidence-based belief
- How detectives evaluate cases
- Why faith should be reasonable

### Chapter 2: Why We Need Evidence
Wallace argues that evidence is essential for belief. He distinguishes between three types of belief:
- Unreasonable belief (against evidence)
- Blind belief (without evidence)
- Forensic belief (based on evidence)

## Key Arguments Summaried

### 1. Faith Should Be Evidence-Based
"We can have a faith that is reasonable, a faith that is based on evidence, a faith that has done its homework."

### 2. The Gospels Are Reliable Documents
"The Gospel writers were eyewitnesses or based on eyewitness accounts. Their testimony is reliable."

### 3. The Resurrection Is the Key
"If the Resurrection didn't happen, Christianity is false. But the evidence for the Resurrection is strong."

### 4. God Is the Best Explanation
"When we examine the evidence for God's existence, theism is the most reasonable conclusion."

## Key Terms

- **Forensic Faith** - Faith grounded in evidence
- **Circumstantial Evidence** - Indirect evidence that requires inference
- **Eyewitness Testimony** - Firsthand accounts from those who saw events
- **Undesigned Coincidences** - Details in accounts that match but weren't intended to
- **Resurrection** - The belief that Jesus rose from the dead
- **Bias** - Preconceived opinions that may affect testimony
- **Corroborating Evidence** - Independent evidence that supports other evidence
