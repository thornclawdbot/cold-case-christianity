## Book Overview

J. Warner Wallace, a cold-case homicide detective, applies his investigative skills to examine the claims of Christianity. He treats the Gospels as "cold cases" and uses forensic principles to determine if they are reliable.

## Key Themes

### 1. Evidence-Based Faith
Wallace argues that faith should be based on evidence, not blind acceptance. He distinguishes between:
- **Unreasonable Belief** - Holding beliefs despite contrary evidence
- **Blind Belief** - Accepting without evidence
- **Forensic Belief** - Evidence-based faith (most reliable)

### 2. Eyewitness Testimony
The Gospels were written by eyewitnesses or based on eyewitness accounts. Wallace examines:
- How eyewitness testimony works in criminal investigations
- Why the Gospel writers' testimonies are reliable
- How to evaluate conflicting eyewitness accounts

### 3. Circumstantial Evidence
The Gospels contain circumstantial evidence that supports their reliability:
- Undesigned coincidences
- Historicity of details
- Eyewitness fingerprints

### 4. The Resurrection
The central claim of Christianity is Jesus' resurrection. Wallace examines:
- The evidence for the empty tomb
- The appearances of Jesus
- Alternative explanations and why they fail

### 5. Bias and Reliability
Wallace addresses the objection that Gospel writers were biased:
- Bias doesn't mean false
- Eyewitnesses can be biased and accurate
- The "admission of fault" principle

## Key Terms

- **Forensic Faith** - Faith grounded in evidence
- **Circumstantial Evidence** - Indirect evidence that requires inference
- **Eyewitness Testimony** - Firsthand accounts from those who saw events
- **Undesigned Coincidences** - Details in accounts that match but weren't intended to
- **Resurrection** - The belief that Jesus rose from the dead
- **Bias** - Preconceived opinions that may affect testimony
